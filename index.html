Lower Back Strain
Recovery Plan

A phased guide for sprinting-related lower back injuries — stretches, strengthening, massage & return-to-play.

Phase 1Days 1–5 · Acute Phase 2Days 6–14 · Subacute Phase 3Days 15–28 · Loading Phase 4Days 28–42 · Running

A lower back strain from sprinting typically involves the erector spinae, quadratus lumborum (QL), or multifidus muscles. In Australian football the combination of explosive acceleration, kicking, and direction changes can overload the lumbar extensors — especially if the glutes aren't firing optimally and the lower back compensates.

⚠ Seek medical attention immediately if you experience numbness or tingling down your legs, loss of bladder/bowel control, or pain that worsens significantly over the first week rather than improving.
Phase 1

Acute

Days 1–5 · Reduce inflammation, protect the area, begin gentle movement

  • Avoid prolonged sitting and bed rest. Alternate between walking, standing, and lying down.
  • Ice 15–20 min on / 40 min off for the first 48–72 hours. After 72 hours, switch to heat or alternate.
  • Heat from day 3–4 onward — moist heat (hot pack, warm shower) for 15–20 min to relax spasm.
  • Walk frequently. Short walks (10–15 min) every few hours.

Gentle Movement

Perform 2–3× daily. Stop any movement that produces sharp pain.

Pelvic Tilts (Supine)

Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Gently flatten your lower back into the floor by tilting your pelvis posteriorly, then release. No forceful pressing.

2 × 10 reps · slow and controlled

Knees-to-Chest (Single Leg)

Gently pull one knee toward your chest with both hands until a mild stretch is felt in the lower back. Keep the opposite leg extended or bent on the floor.

Hold 20–30 sec · 2–3 reps per side

Supine Spinal Rotation

Knees bent, feet flat. Let both knees fall gently to one side, keeping shoulders on the ground. Only go as far as is comfortable.

Hold 20–30 sec each side · 2–3 reps

Cat-Cow

On all fours. Slowly round the back (cat), then gently extend (cow). Keep the range small — you're just mobilizing, not stretching hard.

2 × 8 reps · very gentle
Phase 2

Subacute Recovery

Days 6–14 · Restore mobility, activate glutes & core, soft tissue work

Stretches

Hold each 30–45 seconds, 2–3 sets per side. Perform daily, ideally twice a day.

Hip Flexor / Psoas Stretch

Half-kneeling position. Tuck your pelvis (posterior tilt) and lean gently forward. Feel the stretch in the front of the hip/thigh of the down leg, NOT in the lower back.

Hold 30–45 sec · 2–3 sets per side

Piriformis / Deep Glute Stretch

Lie on your back. Cross one ankle over the opposite knee, then pull the bottom thigh toward your chest. Releases deep external rotators that tighten when the lower back is irritated.

Hold 30–45 sec · 2–3 sets per side

Child's Pose with Lateral Reach

Standard child's pose, then walk both hands to the left to stretch the right QL and lats, hold, then switch. Great for QL release.

Hold 30–45 sec each side · 2–3 sets

Supine Hamstring Stretch

Lie on your back, loop a towel around one foot, gently straighten the leg toward the ceiling. Tight hamstrings pull on the pelvis and increase lumbar load during sprinting.

Hold 30–45 sec · 2–3 sets per side

90/90 Hip Stretch

Sit on the floor, front leg at 90° in front, back leg at 90° behind. Lean your torso forward over the front shin. Opens hip internal and external rotation.

Hold 30–45 sec · 2–3 sets per side

Activation Exercises

Daily. Focus on waking up the glutes and deep core.

Glute Bridge

Lie on your back, knees bent. Drive through heels, squeeze glutes at the top, hold 3 sec. Focus on GLUTES doing the work, not lower back.

3 × 12

Dead Bug

Arms reaching toward ceiling, knees at 90°. Brace your core. Slowly extend one leg out and the opposite arm overhead, return. Keep lower back pressed into the floor.

3 × 8 per side

Bird-Dog

On all fours. Extend one arm forward and the opposite leg back simultaneously. Hold 3–5 sec, return slowly. No rotation of the hips or torso.

3 × 8 per side

Side-Lying Clamshell

Lie on your side, knees bent ~45°, feet together. Open the top knee without rolling your pelvis back. Targets glute medius — critical for single-leg stability during running.

3 × 15 per side

Pallof Press

Stand sideways to an anchored resistance band at chest height. Press the band straight out from your chest and hold 3–5 sec. Resist the rotational pull. Trains anti-rotation core stability.

3 × 10 per side

Massage & Soft Tissue Work

Foam Rolling (daily, 60–90 sec per area)

Thoracic spine (upper back), glutes (cross ankle over opposite knee), hamstrings and hip flexors/quads. Pause on tender spots. Avoid rolling directly on the injured lower back muscles during this phase.

Lacrosse Ball Work

QL release: Ball just above the iliac crest, slightly to the side of the spine — breathe into it for 60–90 sec per side.
Glute trigger points: Sit on a lacrosse ball, find tender spots, hold pressure 30–60 sec.
Piriformis: Same positioning but deeper — closer to the center of the buttock.

Professional Massage / Physio (recommended)

1–2 sports massage sessions focusing on glutes, hamstrings, QL, and thoracic erectors. Ask the therapist to avoid deep direct work on the injury site until pain is below 2/10.

Phase 3

Strengthening & Loading

Days 15–28 · Progressive loading, build posterior chain resilience

Daily pain should be minimal (0–2/10). Perform 3–4× per week. Continue all Phase 2 stretches pre/post workout.

Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

Hinge at the hips, keep a neutral spine, feel the stretch in the hamstrings. Start light (10–15 lb). The single most important exercise for loading the posterior chain without relying on the lower back.

3 × 10

Goblet Squat

Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell at your chest. Squat with full depth (as pain allows). Reinforces proper hip hinge and core bracing under load.

3 × 10

Single-Leg Glute Bridge

Progress from the bilateral bridge. One foot on the ground, opposite leg extended. Drive through the heel, squeeze the glute at the top.

3 × 10 per side

Side Plank

Elbow on the ground, body in a straight line. For progression, lift the top leg and hold. Trains the lateral core and QL in a controlled way.

3 × 30–45 sec per side

Farmer's Carry

Hold heavy dumbbells or kettlebells at your sides, walk with a tall posture. Trains the entire trunk under load in a spine-friendly way.

3 × 40 meter walks

Also continue:

Cable or band pull-throughs (3×12), dead bug progressions with band or weight (3×8/side), all Phase 2 stretches, foam rolling, and lacrosse ball work. Walking and light cycling are fine as cardio.

Warm-Up

Return-to-Play Activation Routine

~12–15 minutes · Before every workout, run, or match

Part 1 — General Warm-Up (3 min)

Light jog or skipping for 2–3 minutes to raise core temperature.

Part 2 — Dynamic Mobility (4 min)

~30 meters or 8–10 reps per side each.

World's Greatest Stretch

Lunge forward, plant your inside hand on the ground next to your front foot, then rotate and reach the other arm to the sky. Incredible hip and thoracic spine opener.

8–10 reps per side

Also include:

Walking Knee Hugs — pull one knee to chest, step forward, alternate.
Walking Quad Pull with Reach — pull heel to glute, reach opposite arm overhead.
Lateral Lunge Walk — step wide to the side, sit into the hip.
Inchworm + Push-Up — walk hands out, push-up, walk feet to hands.
Leg Swings — 10 front-to-back and 10 side-to-side per leg.

Part 3 — Glute & Core Activation (4 min)

Mini-Band Lateral Walk

Band around ankles or just above knees. 10 steps each direction, stay low in a quarter-squat. Fires up the glute medius. Follow with Monster Walks (forward at 45° angles).

10 steps each direction × 2 patterns

Single-Leg Glute Bridge — 8 per side, 2-sec hold at top.
Dead Bug — 5 per side, quick core brace activation.
Pallof Press — 8 per side, quick controlled reps.

Part 4 — Running Build-Up (3 min)

Progressive sprints. Walk back between each as recovery.

2 × 40m at 50% — easy striding, long steps, relaxed
2 × 40m at 70% — increase turnover, stay relaxed through the shoulders
2 × 40m at 85% — near-sprint but controlled, drive through the glutes
1 × 40m at 95% — only if everything feels good, full sprint mechanics

Key Principles to Prevent Reinjury

  • Glutes are the engine, the lower back is the chassis. If the glutes are weak or inhibited, the lower back picks up the slack during sprinting. Maintain glute strength work 2–3× per week permanently.
  • Hip mobility is non-negotiable. Tight hip flexors and hamstrings change pelvic mechanics and load the lumbar spine. Don't skip the warm-up.
  • Brace before you sprint. Engage your deep core (transverse abdominis) before explosive efforts. Think "tighten your belt" before a hard sprint or kick.
  • Manage your training load. The most common cause of muscle strains is spikes in volume/intensity. Don't go from a week off to full-speed match play.
  • Sleep and recovery matter. Soft tissue healing is primarily a nighttime process. Prioritize 7–9 hours and adequate protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight).

This is a general guideline, not medical advice. Listen to your body — if a phase needs an extra week, take it.
Rushing back is the fastest way to re-strain.
