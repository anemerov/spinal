# Lower Back Strain Recovery Plan

---

## Overview

A lower back strain from sprinting typically involves the erector spinae, quadratus lumborum (QL), or multifidus muscles. Sprinting places enormous demand on the posterior chain, and in Aussie rules the combination of explosive acceleration, kicking, and direction changes can overload the lumbar extensors — especially if the glutes aren't firing optimally and the lower back compensates.

> **Important:** If you experience any numbness/tingling down your legs, loss of bladder/bowel control, or pain that worsens significantly over the first week rather than improving, see a sports medicine physician or physio immediately.

---

## Phase 1 — Acute (Days 1–5)

The goal is to reduce inflammation, protect the area, and begin gentle movement.

### What to Do
- **Avoid prolonged sitting and bed rest.** Both stiffen the lumbar spine. Alternate between walking, standing, and lying down.
- **Ice:** 15–20 min on / 40 min off for the first 48–72 hours post-injury if swelling or acute spasm is present. After 72 hours, switch to heat or alternate.
- **Heat:** From day 3–4 onward, moist heat (hot pack, warm shower) for 15–20 min helps relax spasm and improve blood flow.
- **OTC anti-inflammatories** (ibuprofen) as needed for the first few days — follow label directions.
- **Walk frequently.** Short walks (10–15 min) every few hours to keep blood flowing without loading the spine aggressively.

### Gentle Movement (start immediately if tolerable)
Perform 2–3× daily. Stop any movement that produces sharp pain.

1. **Pelvic Tilts (supine)**
   - Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Gently flatten your lower back into the floor by tilting your pelvis posteriorly, then release. No forceful pressing.
   - 2 × 10 reps, slow and controlled.

2. **Knees-to-Chest (single leg)**
   - Same position. Gently pull one knee toward your chest with both hands until a mild stretch is felt in the lower back. Hold 20–30 sec, switch sides.
   - 2–3 reps per side.

3. **Supine Spinal Rotation (knees together)**
   - Knees bent, feet flat. Let both knees fall gently to one side, keeping shoulders on the ground. Only go as far as is comfortable.
   - Hold 20–30 sec each side, 2–3 reps.

4. **Cat-Cow (very gentle)**
   - On all fours. Slowly round the back (cat), then gently extend (cow). Keep the range small — you're just mobilizing, not stretching hard.
   - 2 × 8 reps.

## Phase 2 — Subacute Recovery (Days 6–14)

Pain should be noticeably decreasing. The goal shifts to restoring mobility, beginning light activation, and addressing the hip/glute patterns that likely contributed.

### Stretches (daily, ideally 2×/day)
Hold each 30–45 seconds, 2–3 sets per side.

1. **Hip Flexor / Psoas Stretch (half-kneeling)**
   - Tight hip flexors pull the pelvis into anterior tilt, increasing lumbar extension stress. Kneel on one knee, opposite foot forward. Tuck your pelvis (posterior tilt) and lean gently forward. You should feel the stretch in the front of the hip/thigh of the down leg, NOT in the lower back.

2. **Piriformis / Deep Glute Stretch (figure-4)**
   - Lie on your back. Cross one ankle over the opposite knee, then pull the bottom leg toward your chest. This releases deep external rotators that tighten up to compensate for an irritated lower back.

3. **Child's Pose with Lateral Reach**
   - Standard child's pose, then walk both hands to the left to stretch the right QL and lats, hold, then switch. Great for QL release.

4. **Supine Hamstring Stretch (belt/towel assisted)**
   - Lie on your back, loop a towel around one foot, gently straighten the leg toward the ceiling. Tight hamstrings pull on the pelvis and increase lumbar load during sprinting.

5. **90/90 Hip Stretch**
   - Sit on the floor, front leg at 90° in front, back leg at 90° behind. Lean your torso forward over the front shin. Excellent for opening up hip internal/external rotation.

### Activation Exercises (daily)
Focus on waking up the glutes and deep core — the muscles that should be protecting your spine when you sprint.

1. **Glute Bridge**
   - Lie on your back, knees bent. Drive through heels, squeeze glutes at the top, hold 3 sec. Focus on GLUTES doing the work, not lower back.
   - 3 × 12

2. **Dead Bug (basic)**
   - Lie on your back, arms reaching toward ceiling, knees at 90°. Brace your core (think: belt tightening around your waist). Slowly extend one leg out and the opposite arm overhead, return. Keep lower back pressed into the floor throughout.
   - 3 × 8 per side

3. **Bird-Dog**
   - On all fours. Extend one arm forward and the opposite leg back simultaneously. Hold 3–5 sec, return slowly. No rotation of the hips or torso.
   - 3 × 8 per side

4. **Side-Lying Clamshell**
   - Lie on your side, knees bent ~45°, feet together. Open the top knee without rolling your pelvis back. Targets glute medius, which is critical for single-leg stability during running.
   - 3 × 15 per side

5. **Pallof Press (band)**
   - Stand sideways to an anchored resistance band at chest height. Press the band straight out from your chest and hold 3–5 sec. Resist the rotational pull. Trains anti-rotation core stability.
   - 3 × 10 per side

### Massage & Soft Tissue Work

1. **Foam Rolling (daily, 60–90 sec per area)**
   - Thoracic spine (upper back) — roll up and down, extend over the roller.
   - Glutes — sit on the roller, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, roll the glute.
   - Hamstrings and hip flexors/quads — roll slowly, pause on tender spots.
   - **Avoid foam rolling directly on the injured lower back muscles** during this phase. The area is still healing; direct pressure can re-irritate it.

2. **Lacrosse Ball Work**
   - **QL release:** Lie on your back with a lacrosse ball just above the iliac crest (hip bone), slightly to the side of the spine. Apply gentle pressure and breathe into it for 60–90 sec per side.
   - **Glute trigger points:** Sit on a lacrosse ball on a firm chair or the floor. Find tender spots in the glute and hold pressure 30–60 sec.
   - **Piriformis:** Same as glute work but positioned deeper — closer to the center of the buttock.

3. **Professional Massage / Physio (recommended)**
   - If accessible, 1–2 sports massage sessions during this phase focusing on glutes, hamstrings, QL, and thoracic erectors. Ask the therapist to avoid deep direct work on the acute injury site until pain is below 2/10.

## Phase 3 — Strengthening & Loading (Days 15–28)

By now, daily pain should be minimal (0–2/10). The goal is to progressively load the posterior chain and core to build resilience before returning to running.

### Exercises (3–4× per week)

1. **Romanian Deadlift (RDL) — light dumbbell or kettlebell**
   - Hinge at the hips, keep a neutral spine, feel the stretch in the hamstrings. Start light (10–15 lb) and focus on perfect form. This is the single most important exercise for teaching your body to load the posterior chain without relying on the lower back.
   - 3 × 10

2. **Goblet Squat**
   - Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell at your chest. Squat with full depth (as pain allows). Reinforces proper hip hinge and core bracing under load.
   - 3 × 10

3. **Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
   - Progress from the bilateral bridge. One foot on the ground, opposite leg extended or held at 90°. Drive through the heel, squeeze the glute.
   - 3 × 10 per side

4. **Cable or Band Pull-Through**
   - Mimics the hip hinge pattern with resistance. Excellent for grooving the glute-dominant extension pattern.
   - 3 × 12

5. **Side Plank (with hip abduction for progression)**
   - Elbow on the ground, body in a straight line. Hold 30–45 sec. For progression, lift the top leg and hold. Trains the lateral core/QL in a controlled way.
   - 3 × 30–45 sec per side

6. **Farmer's Carry**
   - Hold heavy dumbbells or kettlebells at your sides, walk with a tall posture. Trains the entire trunk under load in a spine-friendly way.
   - 3 × 40 meter walks

7. **Dead Bug Progression (with band or weight)**
   - Add a light dumbbell in the hands or loop a band around your feet. Increases core anti-extension demand.
   - 3 × 8 per side

### Continue
- All Phase 2 stretches (pre/post workout)
- Foam rolling and lacrosse ball work
- Walking and light cycling are fine as cardio

---

## Phase 4 — Return to Running (Days 28–42)

Begin running ONLY when you can do all Phase 3 exercises pain-free and can sit, stand, bend, and twist without discomfort.

### Running Progression

| Week | Session 1 | Session 2 | Session 3 |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | 5 min jog / 1 min walk × 4 (24 min total) | 6 min jog / 1 min walk × 4 | 8 min jog / 1 min walk × 3 |
| 2 | 10 min continuous jog, rest, 10 min jog | 15 min continuous jog | 20 min continuous jog |
| 3 | 20 min jog + 4 × 50m strides at 70% | 20 min jog + 6 × 50m strides at 75% | 25 min jog + 4 × 80m at 80% |
| 4 | Sport-specific: shuttle runs, change of direction drills at 70–80% | Add kicking drills at moderate intensity | Full training session at 80–90% |

**Rule:** If pain returns above 2/10 during or after a session, drop back one step and repeat it.

## Return-to-Play Warm-Up & Activation Routine

Use this before every workout, run, or Aussie rules session once you're cleared to play. Takes about 12–15 minutes.

### Part 1 — General Warm-Up (3 min)
- Light jog or skipping for 2–3 minutes to raise core temperature.

### Part 2 — Dynamic Mobility (4 min)
Perform each for ~30 meters or 8–10 reps per side.

1. **Walking Knee Hugs** — Pull one knee to chest, step forward, alternate. Opens glutes and lower back.
2. **Walking Quad Pull with Reach** — Pull heel to glute, reach opposite arm overhead, extend through the hip flexor.
3. **Lateral Lunge Walk** — Step wide to the side, sit into the hip, push off. Opens adductors and loads the lateral hip.
4. **Inchworm + Push-Up** — Walk hands out to push-up position, do one push-up, walk feet to hands, stand. Warms up the entire posterior chain.
5. **World's Greatest Stretch** — Lunge, plant inside hand, rotate and reach the other arm to the sky. Incredible hip/t-spine opener.
6. **Leg Swings (sagittal)** — 10 front-to-back swings each leg, progressive range. Mobilizes hip flexors and hamstrings.
7. **Leg Swings (frontal)** — 10 side-to-side swings each leg. Opens adductors and abductors.

### Part 3 — Glute & Core Activation (4 min)

1. **Mini-Band Lateral Walk** — Band around ankles or just above knees. 10 steps each direction, stay low in a quarter-squat. Fires up the glute medius.
2. **Mini-Band Monster Walk** — Band in the same position, walk forward at 45° angles. 10 steps each direction.
3. **Single-Leg Glute Bridge** — 8 per side, 2-sec hold at top. Ensures each glute is firing independently before you sprint.
4. **Dead Bug (5 per side)** — Quick core brace activation. Keep lower back pinned to the ground.
5. **Pallof Press (band, 8 per side)** — Anti-rotation activation. Quick, controlled reps.

### Part 4 — Running Build-Up (3 min)

Progressive sprints to prepare the nervous system for high-speed output.

1. **2 × 40m at 50%** — Easy striding, long steps, relaxed.
2. **2 × 40m at 70%** — Increase turnover, stay relaxed through the shoulders.
3. **2 × 40m at 85%** — Near-sprint but still controlled. Focus on driving through the glutes.
4. **1 × 40m at 95%** — Only if everything feels good. Full sprint mechanics, no holding back.

Walk back between each as recovery.

## Key Principles to Prevent Reinjury

- **Glutes are the engine, the lower back is the chassis.** If the glutes are weak or inhibited, the lower back picks up the slack during sprinting. Maintain glute strength work 2–3× per week permanently.
- **Hip mobility is non-negotiable.** Tight hip flexors and hamstrings change pelvic mechanics and load the lumbar spine. The warm-up routine above addresses this — don't skip it.
- **Brace before you sprint.** Practice engaging your deep core (transverse abdominis) before explosive efforts. Think about "tightening your belt" before a hard sprint or kick.
- **Manage your training load.** The most common cause of muscle strains is spikes in training volume/intensity. If you've had a week off, don't go straight to full-speed match play.
- **Sleep and recovery matter.** Soft tissue healing is primarily a nighttime process. Prioritize 7–9 hours and adequate protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight) to support tissue repair.

## Timeline Summary

| Phase | Timeline | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| 1 — Acute | Days 1–5 | Reduce pain/inflammation, gentle movement |
| 2 — Subacute | Days 6–14 | Restore mobility, activate glutes/core, soft tissue work |
| 3 — Strengthening | Days 15–28 | Progressive loading, build posterior chain resilience |
| 4 — Return to Running | Days 28–42 | Graded running progression, sport-specific drills |
| **Full Return to Play** | **~Day 42+** | **Full training/match play** |

*This is a general guideline. Listen to your body. If a phase needs an extra week, take it — rushing back is the fastest way to re-strain.*
